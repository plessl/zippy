Overview

The ZIPPY project aims at the investigation and development of a dynamically reconfigurable embedded processor architecture. This architecture will integrate blocks of a novel adaptive computing structure with standard processor components such as CPU core, caches, on-chip memories, and controllers. We will follow a new approach for constructing an adaptive computing element by interleaving lines of reconfigurable logic blocks with lines of memory cells. The resulting adaptive processor will target the important application domains of handheld devices and wearable computing systems. Future generation of such devices will greatly extend current capabilities by combining mobile network access with full audio/video communication, organizing and planning tools, and e-commerce applications.
The most important requirements for embedded processors in these domains are:
  • a sufficient performanceto run real-time tasks such as audio and video decoding,
  • a low power consumption to increase battery life time, and
  • low cost, as handhelds and wearables are consumer devices.
Embedded processors for handhelds are typical client processors, where the goal is not to maximize performance but power- and area efficiency. The design challenge for such processors is to achieve the required performance with a small silicon area and moderate clock frequency. Our proposition is that reconfigurable processors, i.e., processors that adapt their hardware structure to different applications, will be able to answer this challenge.

In the ZIPPY project, we pursue following goals:

  • We will define a new benchmark for the embedded application domains of handhelds and wearables. Programs considered are grouped into the three application classes multimedia, cryptography, and communication.
  • We will construct a novel reconfigurable processor that achieves a performance at least one order of magnitude higher than other processor architectures using a comparable amount of silicon area and energy. The performance will be measured in terms of execution time for a selected mix of applications from the target domains.
  • We will turn the developed technology into an Intellectual Property (IP) core. This  "ZIPPY core" will be offered as a soft component that can be parameterized, simulated, synthesized, and integrated with other components to form a system-on-a-chip.
  • We will investigate and develop processor technology that enables handheld devices to exploit the increased bandwidth available in upcoming communication standards. For example, the successor of the European GSM system, UMTS, will allow to implement real-time multimedia applications in small handheld devices.
